Methodology

How the calendar decides what matters

← Back to the calendar

This calendar merges five sources every twelve hours and gives every event a single score from 0 to 100 for public significance. That score is the only thing that decides how large an event appears on the page. It is a set of editorial judgments expressed as arithmetic, and this page exists so you can see the arithmetic and argue with it.

The City Council is deliberately excluded from the City Record results, because Legistar covers it authoritatively and the two would otherwise double-list the same hearing.

The significance score

One scale is used for everything, so that a committee hearing and a World Cup final can sit in the same sorted list and land in the right size of type.

TierScoreMeaning
Marquee85-100The city notices without being told
Major65-84Consequential; it will be covered
Notable45-64Matters to the people it matters to
Routine0-44The machinery of government running
Hearings are capped at 62. The single most consequential committee hearing of the year can reach "notable," but it can never outrank an election, a hurricane anniversary or a World Cup final. This is a deliberate choice. A calendar that ranks by who filed paperwork will always bury the things people actually care about beneath the things government does on a schedule.

How a hearing is scored

A City Council hearing starts at 22 and earns:

  • +28 if it is a stated meeting of the full Council, where legislation is actually passed
  • +10 if it belongs to a committee whose subject reliably reaches the public: public safety, housing, land use, education, health, criminal justice, immigration, transportation, finance
  • +8 if a vote is scheduled
  • +8 if the subject matter matches terms of broad public interest, such as rent, evictions, rezoning, police or the closure of Rikers
  • +6 if it is a joint hearing of more than one committee

A hearing filed in the City Record starts at 20 and is weighted by the agency holding it, on the theory that some bodies decide things people feel directly: the Rent Guidelines Board earns 24, City Planning 18, the Board of Correction 16, Landmarks and the Housing Authority 14.

How an anniversary is scored

Every run, the builder walks 282 sourced historical events and asks which of them fall on a round-number anniversary within the next 400 days. Quarter-centuries and above — 25th, 50th, 75th, 100th and onward — always qualify. Decade anniversaries (10th, 20th, 30th, 40th) also qualify, but only for events that still register with the public, meaning an importance of 3 or higher. Without that bar the calendar fills with the 20th anniversary of things nobody marks. It is the rule that lets the 30th anniversary of the TWA Flight 800 crash and the 10th of the Chelsea bombing appear while the 20th of a middling museum opening does not. A qualifying anniversary scores:

30 + (importance × 10) + roundness + living memory

  • Importance (1-5) is how much the event means to the public today. This is the dominant term.
  • Roundness adds between 4 (a 10th) and 22 (a 300th). It is deliberately a small modifier, not the driver.
  • Living memory adds 8 if the event happened within the last 60 years, on the reasoning that a city remembers what its residents lived through.
Why roundness is kept small. An early draft let the number of zeroes dominate, and the result was that the 250th anniversary of the Battle of White Plains — fought in Westchester — outranked both the general election and the 25th anniversary of 9/11. A 250th is not automatically more significant than a 25th. What the event was matters more than how the arithmetic of the calendar happens to land.

A warning about importance

The importance value is the one number in this system that a machine cannot supply. It is a judgment: 9/11 is a 5, the Triangle Shirtwaist fire is a 5, the Battle of White Plains is a 1. Those judgments live in a single readable file, seed/importance.json, alongside the scale they use, so that a disagreement can be an argument about a specific number rather than a complaint about a black box.

The source corpus ships its own weight field. It is not used, and it is important to say why: it is a display value for a timeline, and it scores 9/11 as a 1 while scoring Studio 54 as a 3. Treating it as significance inverted the entire ranking. It is ignored deliberately.

What this calendar does not know

  • Albany hearings. The state Senate's API publishes floor calendars and an archive of hearing transcripts, but no forward schedule of hearings. Only session days appear here, and only once a year's session is set. When the Legislature is out of session, the state source correctly shows nothing.
  • Anything filed late. Agencies publish hearing notices in the City Record roughly two to three weeks ahead. Something scheduled for next spring by an agency other than the Council will not appear until it is noticed.
  • Dates marked "not yet confirmed." Several curated entries are best estimates based on past practice — the Rent Guidelines Board's votes, the Nunez monitor reports, the first day of school. They are labelled on the page and should not be relied on for planning.
  • Events before today. The page shows only what is ahead.

Promised, but undated — and therefore absent

Several of this administration's signature commitments have no scheduled date, and a calendar should not invent one. They are listed here rather than given a date they do not have:

  • Fare-free buses. No date. Mamdani conceded in April 2026 that it would not happen that year. It needs both action by the transit board and money from Albany.
  • City-owned grocery stores. Year only. Five pilot stores are funded; the first, in Hunts Point, is reported as expected to open in 2027, with East Harlem projected as late as 2029.
  • A Department of Community Safety. What exists is a mayoral office, created by executive order in March 2026. A charter-level department has no date.
  • A millionaire or corporate tax. No date. It requires Albany, so the realistic vehicle is the state budget due April 1, 2027.
  • The Commission on Government Efficiency's final vote. Its hearings are finished, but no vote has been scheduled. The Sept. 4 entry on this calendar is the deadline the law implies for reaching the November ballot, not an announced meeting.

How often it refreshes

A GitHub Action rebuilds the calendar twice a day, at about 6am and 6pm Eastern, and commits the result. The page itself re-checks the feed every ten minutes while open, and offers a reload if a newer build has landed. The two .ics feeds are subscribable: a calendar app that subscribes will keep pulling the updated file on its own, which is the closest thing here to a product that never goes stale.

If every live source fails, the build aborts rather than publishing an empty calendar over a good one.

Your stars and notes

You can star any event and write a private note against it, and both appear under My list, which exports to a calendar file, to plain text, or to a backup file.

None of it leaves your machine. There is no account, no login and no server to send it to: stars and notes are written to your browser's local storage and nothing else. That has an honest downside worth stating plainly — clearing your browsing data will erase them, and they do not follow you to another device or another browser. If you build up a list worth keeping, use the Backup button, which downloads a file with everything you have marked. Notes ride along into the .ics export too, so whatever you wrote shows up in your own calendar app.

AI caution. This calendar was built with AI assistance. The hearings, agendas and session days come directly from government feeds, and the historical events are drawn from a sourced corpus. But the significance scores are editorial judgments expressed as numbers, and any step in an automated pipeline can carry an error. Nothing here should be treated as an official notice of a government proceeding. For the legally operative version of any hearing, consult the City Record or the Council's own calendar.
